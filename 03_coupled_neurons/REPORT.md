# Coupled Neurons Report

## Overview

This exercise studies the behavior of two integrate-and-fire neurons coupled through synaptic conductances. The goal is to understand how excitatory or inhibitory synaptic interactions influence spike timing and whether the two neurons tend to fire in a synchronized way.

The model includes the refractory mechanism through a variable threshold and neglects the adaptation phenomenon, as required by the exercise. Both neurons are stimulated by a constant input current, and the resulting membrane potentials, spike trains, and synaptic state variables are analyzed.

---

## Objective

The purpose of this simulation is to evaluate how two coupled integrate-and-fire neurons respond to constant stimulation when they interact through synaptic conductances.

In particular, the exercise investigates:

- how the membrane potentials evolve over time,
- how spikes generated by one neuron affect the other,
- how the synaptic conductances shape the interaction,
- whether the two neurons become synchronized or remain weakly coupled in time.

---

## Model Description

Each neuron is described by a leaky integrate-and-fire dynamics below threshold. The membrane potential evolves as a passive RC system with an equivalent total conductance that includes both the membrane conductance and the synaptic conductance.

In the implemented code, for each neuron:

- the total conductance is `geq = g + gs`
- the equivalent reversal potential is `E0tot = (g*E0 + gs*Es) / geq`
- the equivalent resistance is `rtot = 1 / geq`
- the asymptotic membrane potential is `Vinf = E0tot + rtot*I`
- the effective membrane time constant is `tau = C * rtot`

The membrane potential is then updated with the exact exponential solution over one integration step:

`V[k+1] = (V[k] - Vinf) * exp(-dt/tau) + Vinf`

This is more accurate than a simple Euler step and is consistent with the standard integrate-and-fire formulation for piecewise constant parameters.

---

## Refractory Mechanism

The refractory period is modeled through a dynamic threshold instead of a fixed threshold.

After a spike:

- the membrane potential is reset,
- the threshold is raised to a high value,
- the threshold then decays exponentially back toward its resting value.

In the code this is represented as:

`Vt[k+1] = (Vt[k] - Vtl) * exp(-dt/taut) + Vtl`

This mechanism makes it harder for the neuron to fire again immediately after a spike and therefore limits unrealistically high firing rates.

---

## Synaptic Coupling

The two neurons are connected through synaptic conductances. When one neuron fires, it opens synaptic channels in the other neuron.

The synaptic state variable decays exponentially in time:

`Ps[k+1] = Ps[k] * exp(-dt/taus)`

At the arrival of a presynaptic spike, the synaptic state is increased instantaneously. This produces a transient conductance change in the postsynaptic neuron.

Depending on the chosen synaptic reversal potential:

- an excitatory synapse tends to depolarize the postsynaptic neuron,
- an inhibitory synapse tends to hyperpolarize it.

This allows the simulation of different coupling conditions, such as excitatory-excitatory, inhibitory-inhibitory, or mixed interactions.

---

## Simulation Setup

The simulation follows the Exercise 4 request:

- two coupled integrate-and-fire neurons,
- constant input current,
- refractory period included through a variable threshold,
- no adaptation,
- synaptic coupling through conductances,
- synchrony evaluated through the spike timing.

Recommended parameters include:

- input current such that `r * i = 25 mV`
- synaptic strength in the range `r * gs,max = 0.5 - 5`
- synaptic time constant `ts = 10 ms`
- synaptic reversal potential `Es = 0 mV` for excitation or `Es = -70 mV` for inhibition
- synaptic opening increment `Dps = 0.03 - 0.6`

These values allow one to explore weak and strong coupling as well as excitatory or inhibitory effects.

## Results

## 1. Membrane Potentials

The first figure shows the membrane potentials of the two neurons as functions of time.

This plot is useful to observe:

- whether both neurons fire repetitively,
- whether the firing periods are similar,
- whether spikes occur at nearly the same times,
- whether one neuron leads or lags the other.

If the two traces show repeated spikes with small temporal separation, the neurons are behaving in a synchronized way. If the spikes occur with different timing or if one neuron suppresses the other, the coupling is weaker or inhibitory.

---

## 2. Overlay of Membrane Potentials

The second figure overlays the two membrane potentials in the same panel.

This representation makes synchrony easier to evaluate visually. When the peaks and resets occur at nearly the same instants, the two neurons are strongly phase-locked. If the peaks are shifted or irregular, synchronization is weaker.

This figure is often the clearest one for a qualitative discussion of coupling.

---

## 3. Spike Trains

The spike-train figure shows the firing instants of both neurons.

This is the most direct way to evaluate synchrony. If the spike times line up closely across the two neurons, the system is synchronized. If spikes alternate, drift apart, or show large delays, synchrony is reduced.

This figure is especially important because spike timing is the main output of the integrate-and-fire model.

---

## 4. Synaptic State Variables

The last figure shows the temporal evolution of the synaptic state variables.

These variables increase when a presynaptic spike arrives and then decay exponentially between spikes. Their behavior explains how coupling is transmitted from one neuron to the other.

A larger or more persistent synaptic variable means stronger effective interaction. Therefore, this figure helps interpret why the neurons may become synchronized, weakly coupled, or desynchronized depending on the parameter values.

---

## Interpretation

The simulation shows how constant input current and synaptic coupling jointly determine the firing behavior of the two neurons.

The main qualitative effects are the following:

- excitatory coupling tends to pull the neurons toward more similar spike timing,
- inhibitory coupling tends to delay or suppress the firing of the postsynaptic neuron,
- stronger synaptic conductance generally increases the interaction between the neurons,
- the variable threshold reduces immediate re-firing and makes the spike train more physiologically realistic.

The degree of synchrony depends on the balance between intrinsic firing tendency and synaptic influence. If coupling is strong enough, the neurons may lock their spike timing; if it is weak or inhibitory, synchrony may be reduced.

---

## Relation to Theory

This exercise is a direct extension of the integrate-and-fire model with ionic conductances.

From the theoretical point of view:

- below threshold, the neuron behaves like a leaky RC circuit,
- the spike itself is treated as a reset event,
- the refractory period is modeled by a dynamic threshold,
- the synaptic effect is represented through a conductance that opens at presynaptic spikes and decays exponentially afterward.

This framework captures the essential timing behavior of coupled spiking neurons while remaining much simpler than the full Hodgkin-Huxley formalism.

---

## Conclusion

This exercise demonstrates that even a simplified integrate-and-fire model can reproduce meaningful interactions between neurons when synaptic conductances are included.

The results show that:

- constant input current can sustain repetitive firing,
- synaptic conductances modify the membrane dynamics of the coupled neuron,
- excitatory and inhibitory couplings produce clearly different timing effects,
- synchrony can be evaluated effectively through membrane-potential overlays and spike trains,
- the variable threshold provides a simple and effective refractory mechanism.

Overall, the model is useful for studying how coupling strength and synapse type influence spike coordination between neurons.
